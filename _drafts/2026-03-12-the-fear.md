I've been staring at a position paper I wrote. It makes claims. Real ones. About cognitive prosthetics, about brain injury, about what AI can do for people whose brains don't work the way they used to. It connects my work to peer-reviewed research from 1999. It names patterns. It proposes a measurement framework. It introduces a concept I call the hugr -- the hybrid mind that forms when a person and their AI agents operate as one cognitive unit.

And I'm scared to publish it.

Not because I'm not sure if it's right. But because I know that it is. Human and adversarial review -- me and five specialist agents tearing it apart from different angles, looking for flaws, testing assumptions, trying to break it. The structure held. The claims are defensible. The data is logged. The system is running. It works.

I'm scared because I know what people will think.

They'll think I typed a prompt and an AI spit out a fancy paper and I'm pretending I did something. They'll see the tools and assume the tools did the thinking. They'll look at the claims and assume the claims came from the machine.

They didn't.

The inflection points in this work came from me walking away from the computer. From lying in bed at 2 AM turning an idea over. From driving to work and realizing that two things I'd been working on separately were actually the same thing. From a feeling I couldn't name until I sat back down and talked it through with my agents. The ideas came from the gaps between sessions, not during them.

The AI didn't tell me that the TBI doesn't just take capabilities. It takes the capacity to acquire capabilities. I felt that for years before I had the words. I tried to go back to university in January 2024. Two classes. Three weeks. I had to quit. Not because the material was hard. Because my brain couldn't hold the learning process long enough to build on it. The path to acquiring new skills was walled off.

That realization -- that bypass is a distinct mechanism, different from restoring a lost skill, different from augmenting beyond human capacity -- came from living with the injury. Not from a model. The model helped me articulate it. The model helped me connect it to Cole's research. The model helped me build the system that proves it works. But the seed was mine. It grew in soil the injury made.

And that's the part that's hard to explain.

When you use AI as a cognitive prosthetic, the line between what you thought and what the system helped you think gets blurry from the outside. From the inside it's clear. I know which ideas I brought to the table. I know which connections I saw first and then validated through the system. I know which moments were mine.

But I can't prove it. Not in a way that satisfies someone who's already decided that AI-assisted means AI-generated. There's no log for the walk to the car where it clicked. There's no commit history for the thought I had in the shower.  This is literally where the whole thing started.  

Three weeks ago. A feeling, a conversation with friends on X Spaces. Then a shower to relax at the end of a long day at my job before bed.  But the feeling was strong and I needed someone to talk to about it. So i asked my agent a few questions and a little back and forth and 3 hours later I had the Ghost Hours Framework and the entrance to the deepest rabbit hole of my life.

Three weeks later I'm still in it. And what I found down there is what scares me.

In 1999, Elliot Cole published research showing that cognitive prosthetics work. Custom software that changes the environment around a brain injury survivor so they can perform functional activities again. It worked. The field stayed small because every prosthesis needed a therapist to build it. The technology couldn't travel without the practitioner.

I built mine without a therapist. I built it with AI agents. And along the way I identified 15 cognitive patterns, built a measurement framework, got it running on someone else's machine, and connected all of it to Cole's peer-reviewed work -- without knowing his work existed until after I'd already built the system.

That convergence is real. I didn't manufacture it. I can't explain it away. Two people, 27 years apart, arriving at the same structural conclusions from opposite sides of the table.

And I'm afraid to say that out loud because I'm a guy from Moose Jaw with a brain injury and no degree. Not a researcher. Not a scientist. Not credentialed to make the claims I'm making.

But the claims don't care about credentials. They care about evidence. And the evidence is logged. Every session. Every ghost hour. Every felt-weight score. Every unlock that shows the prosthetic doing what my brain can't do alone. The data is there.

So here's where I am. I built something. I think it matters. I think it could help other people with brain injuries. I think the framework is sound and the measurement is real and the patterns transfer. I also think people might dismiss it because of how it was built or who built it.

Both of those things can be true at the same time.

I don't know when I'll publish the paper. Maybe soon. Maybe after more data. Maybe after I stop being afraid of what people will think and start remembering why I wrote it.

I wrote it because someone else might need it. And they can't find it if I keep it in a folder on my desktop.

That's the tension. The fear says wait. The reason I built it says go.

I haven't decided yet. But I wanted to say it out loud. Even if only here. Even if nobody reads this.

The work is real. The fear is real. I'll figure out which one gets to drive.
