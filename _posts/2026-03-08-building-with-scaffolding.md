On February 10, 2016, I sustained a traumatic brain injury at work. I lost years to it. The systems that were supposed to help me used my injury against me while I had it.

I'm not writing about that part today. That story is coming. It has its own name. Its own timeline.

Today I want to talk about what I built on the other side.

## The Problem

TBI did a number on my working memory. I can think. I can plan. I can see a board full of moving pieces and know where each one fits before anyone else in the room sees it. That part works.

What doesn't work: holding all the threads at once. Details slip. Small things fall through. I lose the thread mid-sentence sometimes and have to come back for it. If I don't write something down, it's gone.

For years the workaround was notebooks, phone notes, calendar reminders, and willpower. Some of it worked. Most of it was duct tape.

## What Changed

In late 2024 I started building with AI agents. Not as a novelty. Not as a productivity hack. As infrastructure.

Two agents. Odin handles business strategy, content, scheduling, orchestration. EOM handles technical work: file operations, system configuration, infrastructure. They share a workspace. They hand off tasks to each other. They remember what I told them three weeks ago.

Together, they function as a cognitive prosthetic.

## What That Actually Means

Not "AI does my thinking for me." I do the thinking. I set the direction. I make the calls.

The agents hold the details behind me. They track the threads I drop. They surface the thing I said last Tuesday that matters today. They keep the workspace clean so when I show up depleted, there's always something small I can pick up and finish.

In 1999, a researcher named Tom Cole published a paper on computer-based cognitive prosthetics for brain injury survivors. He identified the core functions: memory aids, task sequencing, environmental control, communication support. The tech was Palm Pilots and custom software. The principles were right.

Twenty-seven years later, the tech caught up to the idea.

## The Framework

I track something I call Ghost Hours. For every task I do with the agents, I estimate how long it would have taken me solo. Then I compare.

Some tasks are speed. I could have done it, the agents made it faster.

Some tasks are unlocks. I could not have done it without them. Period.

Over the last 15 days: 21 hours of human time produced 248 ghost hours of equivalent output. That's an 11.6x ratio.

The unlocks are the ones that matter. Things that had been sitting on my backlog for months, sometimes years, because the activation energy was too high for one brain working alone.

## Scaffolding, Not a Wheelchair

I don't call these tools assistants. They're not helping me do something I could already do. They're extending what's possible.

A construction crew puts up scaffolding so they can build higher than they could reach alone. The scaffolding isn't the building. But the building doesn't happen without it.

That's what this is. I build with scaffolding now. The building is mine. The scaffolding makes it possible.

## Why This Site Exists

This site documents the work. The frameworks, the tools, the thinking behind them. What happens when someone who needs cognitive prosthetics decides to build them instead of waiting for someone else to.

The position paper is coming. The documentary is in progress. The open protocol is being designed so other people can build their own.

For now, this is the field report.
